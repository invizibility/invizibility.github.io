EEG Visualizer

2014
Processing, Java, R
https://github.com/invizibility/timerangeslider/tree/master

Description

I worked on this project during my Design Technologist/Creative Coding internship at data visualization studio Pitch Interactive during spring of my senior year at UW-Madison. I flew out to San Francisco at the beginning of the year to intern at Pitch for 3 months, wanting to get hands on experience in a data visualization studio environment.

Capacitor is a dance troupe that is interested in using EEG data from their performers to drive a visual projection on the ceiling of their performance space as they perform. Wes, the Director of the studio, assigned this project to me as way of exploring my interest in scientific data visualization with an artistic context. Creating this visualization, I learned how to engage with raw scientific data sets with a combination of basic statistical analysis and visualization design methodology. Additionally, I learned how data visualization can be integrated into artistic practices that engage with flows of information quantifying the human body.

Process

Wes gave me some EEG data from the dancers and asked me to design a Processing application that would explore the patterns created by fluctuations at each electrode mapped to a series of pulsating shapes. First I had to extensively clean and normalize the .csv data in the raw EEG format in order to make the visual mapping easy and performant–I took care of this in R. Next, to allow smooth interaction with the time series data, I created a forked version of Till Nagel’s “Animated Time Range Slider for Processing” that supports nanosecond durations and non-range time increments.

Finally I put my new slider and normalized data together in an interface allows the user to assign a custom shape/color/rotation/pulse behavior to each electrode, and explore patterns created by various combinations of electrodes over the course of their respective time series. The slider was made publically available on my personal github (see above). This sketch was used for introductory data exploration; information about the final version of the project (completed after my internship was over) can be seen here.
