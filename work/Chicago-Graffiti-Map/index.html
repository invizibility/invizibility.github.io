Chicago Graffiti Map

2013
QuantumGIS, Leaflet.js, MapBox, TileMill, Adobe Illustrator
http://bit.do/ChiGraffMap

Description

I created this map in Robert Roth’s “Graphic Design and Cartography” class my senior year at UW-Madison. Students were required to create a interactive tiled map in a web browser overlaid with a “points of interest” dataset. Each type of point of interest was to be denoted with a unique, custom designed icon. I chose to visualize different types of graffiti that are present throughout the city of Chicago. This project helped me learn how to use a variety of GIS tools in and out of the web browser to geographically visualize a dataset. I gathered and parsed data relevant to my visualization question, created map tiles to optimally display the layers of data I wanted to represent, created a custom iconset, and served the map through a JavaScript application.

Process

I was interested in the codified distinction between “street art” and “graffiti”. Through my initial research, I found an interesting juxtaposition of data sources describing geocoded locations of various graffiti types. These included: the City of Chicago’s 311 Service Request Data Portal, which shows all the graffiti removal requests in the past years, Red Bull’s StreetArtView , which shows user submitted geocoded graffiti pictures, and the Chicago Graffiti and Street Art Flickr Group. I collected and cleaned a large sample spanning all three sources. The Flickr and RedBull Data I organized by type as a list of points, and the 311 data I turned into a heatmap which would render as a layer above my Chicago city shapefile. I then experimented with various color schemes and map tile styles in QuantumGIS to present my map in the clearest way at multiple scales.

When I decided on the final shapefile style and heatmap color scheme, I created a custom iconset for the portion of my data organized by type and exported my finalized custom tiles to MapBox. My tiles are currently publically hosted on MapBox.

Although this data was not a random sample, it got me thinking about the emergent patterns of graffiti throughout the city; wheatpasting and commissioned murals tend to be in the hipster neighborhoods while the gang territory signs are more common in less socioeconomically privileged areas.
