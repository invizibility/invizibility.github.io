Background & Prior Work

2016-09-18

There are several different types of media that need to be explored to consolidate a history of prior work in genomics visualization.

  • media consisting of visualizations that describe a problem, process, or observation relating to genomic data.
  • media describing the problem space of visualization in genomics. this has to do with genomics’ place within the epistemological regime of systems biology
  • media that attempts to create criteria for good/bad visualizations

In this post I will tackle the first area. I’ve tried to select visualizations that I think are either effective or ineffective for notable reasons; there are a lot of bad visualizations out there so it is important to learn from ‘near misses’ instead of trying to extract meaning from something that is a complete failure.

This blog post by Mo Rahman, the founder of GeneDrop, provides a good overview of the most common types of visual grammar used in contemporary genomics problem solving. Going through each of the 5 categories outlined in his post, I’ve aggregated some visualization strategies I think are interesting.

Track Visualization

The UCSC genome browser is considered the standard of this type of visualization. This project began to showcase the initial findings of the Human Genome Project, and continues to be a widely used repository for genomic alignment and annotation data for several different species.

Track visualizations present sequential information in a horizontal extended track, and invite users to compare alignments between multiple sequences, or annotations (such as measurement of gene expression, or presence of a promoter region) along the distance of the sequence. This is useful for looking up the significance of a particular region on a genome, but for asking questions about complex conserved trends across species or generations, simple linear track visualization becomes complicated. The following figure shows similarity and inversion of particular regions across 11 Lactobacillus genomes represented as tracks, from the paper “Tracing lifestyle adaptation in prokaryotic genomes”. It gets quite messy.

As such, researchers have explored custom track visualization solutions when a linear genome representation is required. A couple interesting examples include:

GeneDrop Custom Browser

This browser was shown by Mo Rahman on twitter, and is used in house at GeneDrop to analyze structural variations in certain parts of the genome. While this is just a ‘sneak peak’ view that doesn’t extensively reveal any functionality, it offers a perspective of what a genome browser could look like if built with an emphasis on legible and extensible design methodologies. It looks nicer than the UCSC browser, but we don’t have an idea of how the visual design is leveraged into extra functionality because its still proprietary.

Sequence Surveyor

This tool was developed at the Computer Graphics departmartment at the University of Wisconsin-Madison as a scalable solution for comparing and clustering alignments. It uses gradients and cluster averaging to provide a way to compare multiple sequence alignments like in the Lactobacillus example, without the spiderweb of edges required to show congruence/inversion. This tool actually actually has interesting applications in text corpus analysis, but that can be covered later
